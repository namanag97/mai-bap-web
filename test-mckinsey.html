The mathematics of build vs. buy in process intelligence

At low complexity, BI tools are sufficient. At scale, the total cost of ownership crosses over — and the calculus changes entirely.

NA Naman Agarwal · Founder & CEO · Feb 24, 2026 · 8 min read

Every VP of Operations eventually asks the same question: why pay for a dedicated process mining platform when we already have a BI stack? The answer is a crossover function — and knowing where your organisation sits on that curve determines the right answer.

The total cost of ownership model

Building process intelligence on top of a BI stack is not free. It requires sustained engineering investment in ETL pipelines, data modelling, and custom conformance logic. As process complexity grows — more variants, more systems, more edge cases — this cost grows exponentially.

A dedicated PM platform, by contrast, is priced linearly. The base cost is higher, but the marginal cost of adding complexity is near-zero. The two curves cross at a predictable point.

The crossover point is not a fixed number. It is a function of three variables: process scope (S), process variability (V), and the number of integrated systems (Q). Organisations with narrow, stable, single-system processes rarely cross it. Organisations running end-to-end, multi-system, frequently changing processes almost always do.

Calculating the Delta NBV

To make the build-vs-buy decision rigorous, we model the net business value differential — the delta between what PM recovers versus what BI recovers, net of platform cost.

When Δ NBV is positive, the PM platform creates more value than it costs relative to the BI alternative. When it is negative, BI tooling is the rational choice. The framework removes the politics from the decision.
